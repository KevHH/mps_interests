The current of version of the Register of Interests, released on the 15th June 2019, shows that 90% of MPs are declaring some form of outside interest, ranging from innocuous entries like volunteer work to declaring thousands of pounds in additional earnings from second jobs. This is not a problem specific to one party, MPs from some parties do earn less outside of Parliament and some parties do have stricter rules than others about accepting outside money, but the majority of MPs are declaring something which could “reasonably” be seen to influence their actions. Working out the real extent of these declarations is a challenge in and of itself, but even the first step of accurately reading the entries in the Register is a difficult task. The data is hard to decipher, there is no set format for declarations, some MPs give detailed entries others are relatively vague. Some MPs report gross income some net, some annual incomes others monthly or quarterly. There's no formal auditing process involved, the details have to be taken at face value or investigated privately and then taken up with the Commissioner for Standards. Altogether, this makes it difficult to get an overview of exactly what MPs earn outside of Parliament, how much time they spend working other jobs and exactly what they are doing. Yet through scraping the data it's possible to make some estimations about things such as MPs outside employment, their property interests and the gifts they receive as part of their parliamentary work. Looking at this data altogether provides an insight into the nature of the problem and the extent of how outside interests affect the political process in the UK.
