Outside Interests

What the Register of MPs' Interests reveals about the transparency of UK Parliament.

by Michael Keith | | 15th July 2019

"Holders of public office should take decisions solely in terms of the public interest. They should not do so in order to gain financial or other material benefits for themselves, their family, or their friends.

Members shall fulfil conscientiously the requirements of the House in respect of the registration of interests in the Register of Members’ Financial Interests."

- House of Commons Code of Conduct (1st August 2018)

“The only principle of payment in the public service is that you should make an allowance to a man to enable him to maintain himself comfortably and honourably, but not luxuriously, during the time he is rendering service to the State.” This was the opinion of David Lloyd George as he made the case to Parliament that MPs should be granted a fixed salary so that they could focus more on their roles as elected representatives and less on their outside professions. Over a century later the idea of paying salaries to MPs is widely accepted, yet many have not adopted the humble comfort and honour envisaged by Lloyd George. MPs still hold jobs outside of Parliament and many have large business interests or benefit in one way or another from the perks of their political lives. The potential for conflict of interests to arise in these circumstances has been understood since the inception of Parliament and various measures have been taken to ensure transparency from elected representatives. Currently, the main method is to have all MPs declare, in the Register of Members' Interests, anything which could “reasonably be thought by others to influence” their actions, speeches or behaviour in Parliament. The Register is intended to be a comprehensive, publicly available document allowing anyone to investigate the financial dealings of MPs and ensure the accountability and transparency of the political system. However, this is not always the case. Far from ensuring that MPs are free from outside interference, the Register reveals the role outside business dealings have on UK politics and how easy it is for MPs to avoid public accountability on these issues.

The current version of the Register of Interests, as of the 17th June 2019, shows that 90% of MPs (587 out of 650) are declaring some form of outside interest, this could include anything from paid employment, shareholdings, gifts, donations, property ownership or foreign visits. The extent and value of these interests varies significantly between MPs, but the number of declarations show that this is not a problem specific to one party. The majority of MPs are declaring something which could reasonably be seen to influence their actions.

The Register is also not an infallible source. Working out the real extent of these declarations is a challenge in itself, even the first step of accurately reading the entries is difficult. The data is hard to decipher, there is no set format for declarations, some MPs give detailed entries, others are relatively vague. Some MPs report gross income, some net. Some report annual figures, others monthly or quarterly. There's also no formal auditing process, the details have to be taken at face value or investigated privately and then taken up with the Commissioner for Standards. Altogether, this makes it near impossible to get a complete and accurate overview of exactly what MPs earn outside of Parliament, how much time they spend working other jobs and exactly what they are doing. Yet through scraping the data it's possible to make some estimations about MPs' outside employment, their property interests and the gifts they receive as part of their parliamentary work..

“You'd be surprised how much free time I have” said Sir Malcolm Rifkin, the then Conservative MP for Kensington, in 2015, “I am self employed, so nobody pays me a salary, I have to earn my income, but when I'm not doing something I can do what I like.” Rifkin made these comments in a meeting with representatives from what he believed to be a Chinese firm, in reality he was speaking to undercover reporters from Channel 4 and the Daily Telegraph as part of a sting operation exploring how willing MPs would be to promote foreign companies in exchange for payment.

Rifkin's comments laid bare the carefree attitudes some MPs have towards their jobs as public representatives. Ed Miliband, then labour leader, challenged then Prime Minister David Cameron over the affair at the next Prime Minister's Questions, demanding that the Government ban the practice of MP's taking certain second jobs. In a subsequent vote on whether to allow MPs to hold directorship and consultancy positions, the House of Commons voted 289 to 219 against the ban. Many opposing MPs claimed the motion was too vague and that it would deter certain people from entering politics. As William Hague put it “Does it mean... that someone who sets up their own business, succeeds with it, creates jobs and contributes to the British economy is then to be barred from the House of Commons?” He summed up the proposal as “calculated to create a headline rather than to solve a problem.”

Four years later little has changed. Many MPs still hold jobs outside of Parliament and see no problem with the status quo. As of June 2019, 287 out of 650 MPs are declaring some form of paid outside employment, over £100, in the Register of Interests. The nature and pay for these positions varies widely, from small fees for writing occasional newspaper articles, to six-figure salaries for directorship positions, but with any payment there's a possibility it may affect an MPs decision making.

It's easy to see how conflicts of interest could arise here, especially as the entries in the Register don't specify exactly what MPs are doing at these jobs and large sums of money are involved. For instance, Sir Nicholas Soames holds a non-executive position at GardaWorld (formerly known as AEGIS Defence Services Ltd) a company which allegedly benefited greatly from the Iraq war. Yet Soames often raises questions in Parliament regarding defence and army spending. Soames position at GardaWorld alone pays him approximately £110,000 annually. Jacob Rees Mogg spends 30 hours a month working as a partner for his firm Somerset Capital Management LLP and has frequently had to deny claims that his position at the investment firm presents a potential conflict of interest with his political career.

The arguement that restricting MPs from holding second jobs would prevent those in certain profession, such as those who've started a business, or doctors, nurses or barristers from entering politics is not reflected in the data. While a handful of MPs maintain their previous professions, sometimes to retain professional licenses, the majority of MPs who work outside jobs, and make a lot of money doing so, work in either executive directorship or other consultancy positions.

Yet this isn't entirely an issue about the amount of money MPs earn, it also raises the question of how seriously they take their position position as an elected representative. This is perhaps best demonstrated by Douglas Ross, the Scottish Conservative MP for Moray, who moonlights outside of parliament as an assistant football referee for both the Scottish Football Association and UEFA. As an assistant referee Ross earned approximately £22,000 in the last 12 months, working 2 – 3 times per month. This could arguably be considered perfectly acceptable, if it weren't the case that Ross has missed parliamentary sessions in favour of refereeing football games. This point was brought up in Parliament by SNP MP John McNally and Ross subsequently promised to give up refereeing when Parliament was sitting. However many MPs still criticised Ross for having taken so long to realise that his job as an MP outweighed his position as an assistant referee. It seemed that, until it was brought to the attention of the rest of Parliament and the press, Ross was happy to forgo his duties as an MP to attend football matches.

“I think that second jobs should not be allowed” says Martin Williams, “It's not about the case by case basis, what actually matters is the principle and the principle is: could they feasibly be influenced by the pay cheque?” Williams is an investigative journalist who's spent the past few years writing about the outside interests of MPs. In 2016 he wrote the book Parliament Ltd, a detailed and thorough exploration of the public and private financial dealings of MPs and Lords. His conclusion was that many MPs were treating Parliament as a business, using their position to get rich right under the nose of their constituents and often fully within the rules. “At the moment there's loads and loads of stuff that MPs can legitimately get away with not reporting” he says, “things that you or I would say 'well how dare you not report that' but they can perfectly well turn around and say 'well it's not in the rules, so why should I?'” Currently MPs only have to declare certain interests if they meet specific criteria. For example shareholdings only need to be declared if they account for more than 15% of the total issued shares of the company or have a value of over £70,000, but this valuation only has to be done once a year. Add to this the issue that the rules are often not strictly enforced and punishments for failing to disclose information often amount to little more than making a brief apology. “That is what undermines public trust in politics” say Williams, “it's the idea that MPs might not care about the people they represent, that it might not be their first priority, they might have another primary or vested interest.”

In 2016, Parliament was voting on various amendments to the Housing and Planing Bill. Labour MP and the shadow housing minister, Theresa Pearce, proposed an amendment, adding a clause which would “...place a duty on landlords to ensure that their properties are fit for habitation when let and remain fit during the course of the tenancy.” The amendment failed to pass a vote in the Commons, with Conservative MPs voting with their party against the Labour amendment. Yet controversy arose when it was found that 72 of the Conservative MPs who voted down the clause were landlords themselves. Many critics pointed out this potential conflict of interest and suggested MPs were using their position to vote in favour of their own financial interests.

Yet following a brief outcry, little changed. Currently, almost 1 in 5 MPs (120 out of 650) are landlords, receiving at least £10,000 a year in income from rental property. Many MPs own more than one property and some have large buy-to-rent portfolios. The vast majority of these properties are in London, but certain other areas have been a focus point for entrepreneurial MPs seeking to make some additional income from the expanding rental market.

For instance, Jeremy Hunt registered seven apartments, owned through Mare Pond Properties Ltd, a company he jointly owns with his wife, in Southampton, a short distance away from his Constituency of South West Surrey.

Cornwall also appears to be a popular holiday destination for MPs with many registering holiday homes in the areas. Adrian Bailey, the Labour MP for West Bromwich West, appears to like Cornwall so much that he owns two “holiday homes” in the area.

Nadhim Zahawi, the Conservative MP for Stratford-on-Avon and one of the founders of YouGov, is the MP who currently owns the most property of all MPs, declaring 12 properties across the UK, with some sources claiming that Zahawi's property portfolio has a value of over 25 million pounds. All of these properties are rented out through Zahawi & Zahawi Ltd, a property company Zahawi founded with his wife.

In fact the property management business is one of the most popular industries in which MPs find outside employment and often drifts into MPs work in Parliament. For instance, Sir Gary Streeter, the Conservative MP for South West Devon, was one of 10 MPs, who, in June 2018 called for Theresa May to support alternative approaches to solve issues with Britain's housing market. The main idea championed by Sir Streeter was the rent-to-buy scheme offered by the company Rentplus. Sir Gary Streeter has some history with the company, attending many of their events and appearing their promotional videos. In 2016, Sir Gary Streeter spoke about the company in the House of Commons calling it: “...a very innovative scheme and the kind of product I am sure the Government would want to promote.” At a Rentplus event for completion of new properties in Plymouth, Sir Streeter said, “The sooner the Rentplus model is rolled out throughout the UK, the better for the coming generation.” Four months later Sir Streeter has now joined the company as a non-executive director, seemingly happy to convert his previous support for the company into a pay-cheque of £30,000 per year for 20 hours of work a month.

Employment and property interests provide an obvious means through which the impartiality of MPs can be compromised. However there are more subtle factors which can influence MPs decision making. Various companies, lobbying firms and foreign governments frequently pay for MPs to travel to meeting and events outside the UK. Sometimes these are perfectly legitimate trips which form a vital part of MPs work, but other times the purpose of these trips is less clear.

In November 2017, Priti Patel lost her job as international development secretary after conducting several secret meetings with Israeli officials. Labour leader Jeremy Corbyn also faced criticism for not declaring a trip to Israel he made in 2010.

The places MPs are paid to visit reveals a lot about UK relations with other countries. Unsurprisingly the US is the top destination for travelling MPs, but areas like China and India are also popular destinations. Israel and Palestine are also two of the most popular areas, with these trips often funded by a variety of lobbying organisations keen to connect MPs with officials in the area.

The Government of Gibraltar is the organisation which has provided foreign visits to the most UK MPs, paying for 18 MPs to fly and stay in Gibraltar for various reasons during the past year.

Following complaints by various newspapers and campaign groups, MPs are now wary of visits to places like Saudi Arabia. Once quite a popular destination for MPs, the number of visits has declined in the past 12 months largely due to the assassination of Jamal Khashoggi. Interestingly, MPs who were paid to visit Saudi Arabia have been quite effective at removing this information from the Register of Interests. The latest version of the Register only claims to display visits in the past year, but some older visits do remain on more recent version. However, visits to controversial destinations are often removed as soon as possible.

This is just a fraction of the list of declarations made by MPs. Understanding and uncovering the full extent of MPs' outside financial involvements would require hours of further investigative work. Equally, the Register is not reliable, MPs often fail to make declarations on time or provide the necessary information. The inaccessibility of the register accompanied by this lax enforcement of the rules contributes to a wider lack of transparency and general distrust in politics as a whole.

Since the expenses scandal in 2009, this lack of trust has been something that Parliament has been keen to address. In 2018, the Committee on Standards in Public Life released it's 19th report on the subject of MPs' outside interests. The report made several recommendations specifically regarding the Register of Interests and it's accessibility, namely: “As a matter of urgency, the Register of Members’ Interests should be updated to ensure it is digitally accessible to the public and other MPs.” Other recommendations involved reviewing and updating the code of conduct to better inform MPs of the requirements on declaring interests. Yet despite this being a “matter of urgency” nothing has yet been done to make the Register more accessible or improve the methods or accuracy of reporting.

"...there are people that are preventing change out of their own interests... It doesn't interest them to have a more accountable parliament.”

- Martin Williams, author of Parliament Ltd

This isn't the first time the critics have suggested that changes be made to the Register. In 2016, the Select Committee for Standards in Public Life held a session where various academics and journalists gave their feedback and opinions on the improvements which could be made to the transparency of parliament. Martin Williams was present at the meeting and pressed the Committee on the need for greater transparency of the system as a whole. Yet, nothing came of these suggestions. Williams has a theory about why change in this area might be so slow. “I think I, quite naively, went in [to the committee meeting] thinking they honestly wanted to do something... and I came out and thought 'ah, they don't actually care.'” Williams feels that the committee itself isn't particularly interesting in making any real changes, “I felt that they were almost wilfully blind to solutions” he says, “but to be honest the entire credibility of the committee is completely undermined by the fact that multiple members have been investigated for failing to declare stuff.” Williams is careful not to name anyone specifically here, but some committee members have been found in breach of the rules, most notably Geoffrey Cox, who, in 2016, failed to declare £400,000 of outside earnings. After investigating, the committee found that no conflict of interests was posed from Cox's failure to declare the income and chose not to formally punish the MP. Williams sees this as a key part of the problem, “when I was on the committee...” says Williams, “obviously when you're speaking in Parliament you're under Parliamentary privilege, therefore you can call certain people corrupt and not fear libel, there are people I would have called corrupt and I wish I had... there are people that are preventing change out of their own interests... It doesn't interest them to have a more accountable parliament.”

Beyond the rules about MPs behaviour, the ultimate test should be whether or not the electorate really care about what interests their elected representatives hold outside of Parliament. Cases like the expenses scandal prove that this is an issue the public care to some extent, but it's difficult to test whether this really affects they was in which people vote.

Some studies have attempted to access the impact outside financial interests actually have on voting behaviour or public perception of MPs. In writing their report, the Committee for Standards in Public Life used analysis from several studies, alongside holding several roundtable discussions to understand how much these factors really altered voting behaviour. Professor Rosie Campbell, of King's College London, is the co-author of one of these studies and attended these meetings. Professor Campbell, along with her colleagues, analysed the results of various surveys where respondents were asked to choose between two different candidates. The only information provided about the candidates was their profession and how much money they made. Summarising the outcomes Professor Campbell says, “[We] found in our research that wealthy candidates are less attractive to voters generally. Candidates who weren't independently wealthy were more popular.. but [the survey] was quite an artificial environment.” Speculating on the main concerns voters may have she says “what often there's an objection to is politicians who gain a lot of wealth through their political life rather than their other life.” One theory behind how people choose who to vote for is that they tend to support candidates they perceive to be like themselves, “...it might be social distance” say Professor Campbell, “the wealthier candidates were less attractive [in the survey environment] because the respondent to the survey has to make a choice between which candidates they prefer and they're going to find it easier if they get a cue that potentially reduces the social distance between themselves and the candidate.”

"...it's not realistic to think that this will inform [ordinary voters] behaviour without being mediated by someone who can work out what's a story and what isn't.."

- Prof. Rosie Campbell, Kings College London

Yet professor Campbell is hesitant to suggest that the findings of the surveys would have any real impact on voter behaviour and that a range of other factors are important to understanding how the electorate make decisions, particularly which party they represent. Professor Campbell ultimately believes that, in general, the public are only interested in the bigger financial scandals involving MPs and aren't too concerned about the relative income or wealth of their representatives unless it presents an immediate conflict of interest. “The good thing about the Register [of Members' of Interests] isn't really that ordinary voters, or very many of them, are going to read this stuff, it's that journalists will and if it's shocking they'll try and make a story out of it. It's not realistic to think that this will inform their behaviour without being mediated by someone who can work out what's a story and what isn't.”

Martin Williams, someone who Professor Campbell might deem fit to “work out what's a story and what isn't”, acknowledges this view as well, “A lot of the time stuff like this is reported, but it doesn't make the front page” he says, “people are not necessarily on the lookout for it.” Yet, his view is slightly more optimistic about the public's interest, “I think most people want to know who's paying their MPs, what their their MPs are doing 9-5... there's this whole other issue of whether or not knowing that stuff actually changes people's voting habits. The conclusion I arrived at was it doesn't necessarily... part of that is because a lot of parties are bad for it, so it's not like you can single one out... so it makes it very difficult for this to become an election issue.”

Regardless or the real impact, it seems unlikely that MPs will vote to tighten rules on outside interests anytime soon. In the meantime, the only realistic approach to keeping Parliament transparent is to keep trawling through the Register of Interests and holding MPs accountable for what they declare outside of Parliament.
